ELJDO Learning Arc

AI Literacy Dimensions for Educators

E — Experience
Functional Dimension
▼

Core Question

What happens when I encounter AI?

AI Literacy Sub-Skills

1. Recognizing AI
• Identify when AI is present in tools and systems
• Distinguish between AI types (generative, algorithmic, predictive)
• Understand what different AI tools actually do
2. Understanding AI Capabilities & Limitations
• Know what AI can and cannot do reliably
• Recognize AI's strengths (pattern recognition, generation, synthesis)
• Recognize AI's weaknesses (hallucination, bias, context-blindness)
3. Experiential Knowledge of AI Behavior
• Learn through direct interaction how AI responds
• Recognize patterns in AI outputs (when it succeeds/fails)
• Develop intuition about AI's tendencies and limitations

Research Framework Alignment

Long & Magerko (2020): "What is AI?" and "What can AI do?"
Ng et al. (2021): "Know and recognize AI"

L — Language
Rhetorical Dimension
▼

Core Question

How do I communicate my thinking to AI?

AI Literacy Sub-Skills

1. Prompt Engineering / Prompting Competence
• Formulate clear, specific requests to AI
• Provide context, constraints, and examples
• Iterate and refine prompts based on output
2. Communicating Intent & Context
• Make goals explicit to AI
• Specify desired format, tone, and level
• Articulate what you DON'T want AI to do
3. Understanding AI as Communicative Partner
• Recognize how AI interprets language probabilistically
• Understand that vague input → vague output
• Treat prompting as instruction/teaching

Research Framework Alignment

Cardon et al. (2023): "Prompting and prompt patterns"
Long & Magerko (2020): "How AI works" (language models)
Ng et al. (2021): "Use and apply AI"

J — Judgment
Critical & Ethical Dimension
▼

Core Question

Should I trust, use, or question this output?

AI Literacy Sub-Skills

1. Critical Evaluation of AI Outputs
• Assess accuracy, relevance, and reliability
• Detect hallucinations, fabrications, and errors
• Compare AI output to authoritative sources
2. Recognizing AI Bias & Limitations
• Identify bias in training data and outputs
• Understand systemic limitations (knowledge cutoffs, demographic gaps)
• Question whose perspectives are represented/missing
3. Ethical Reasoning about AI Use
• Consider fairness, privacy, and accountability
• Evaluate impact on stakeholders (students, colleagues, society)
• Make value-based decisions about when NOT to use AI
4. Verification & Fact-Checking Practices
• Cross-reference AI claims with reliable sources
• Apply epistemic standards (evidence, traceability, authority)
• Understand burden of verification when using AI
5. Understanding AI's Social & Cultural Impact
• Recognize how AI shapes information access
• Consider implications for equity and power
• Understand AI's role in amplifying or mitigating inequality

Research Framework Alignment

Long & Magerko (2020): "Critical perspective on AI" and "AI ethics"
Ng et al. (2021): "Evaluate and create AI" and "AI ethics"
Southworth et al. (2023): "Critically engaging with AI systems"

D — Design
Pedagogical Dimension
▼

Core Question

How can I use AI pedagogically without losing control?

AI Literacy Sub-Skills

1. Pedagogical Integration of AI
• Design learning activities that use AI appropriately
• Align AI use with learning objectives
• Ensure AI supports rather than replaces thinking
2. Task Design for AI-Enhanced Learning
• Create tasks where AI role is clear and bounded
• Scaffold AI use for student learning
• Design assessments that reveal understanding (not just AI-assisted output)
3. Understanding AI's Role in Learning Processes
• Know when AI helps learning vs. hinders it
• Recognize cognitive load implications
• Understand transfer and dependency risks
4. Instructional Decision-Making with AI
• Choose appropriate AI tools for specific learning goals
• Decide what should remain human-mediated
• Balance efficiency with pedagogical quality
5. Creating AI Literacy Learning Experiences
• Teach students to use AI critically and responsibly
• Design opportunities for students to develop AI competencies
• Model thoughtful AI integration

Research Framework Alignment

Southworth et al. (2023): "Design and use of AI in education"
Cardon et al. (2023): "AI for teaching and learning"
Ng et al. (2021): "Create with AI" (educational context)

O — Own
Agential Dimension
▼

Core Question

What is my professional stance on AI?

AI Literacy Sub-Skills

1. Metacognitive Awareness of AI Use
• Reflect on how AI affects your thinking and work
• Recognize when you're over-relying on AI
• Understand your own learning/working process with AI
2. Professional Agency & Responsibility
• Maintain ownership of outputs regardless of AI involvement
• Take accountability for quality and accuracy
• Claim authorship and professional judgment
3. Ethical Stance & Professional Identity
• Articulate personal/professional values regarding AI
• Make principled decisions about AI use
• Position yourself in relation to AI (tool user, not tool-dependent)
4. Self-Regulation of AI Use
• Set boundaries on when/how to use AI
• Monitor dependency and maintain independent capability
• Develop sustainable practices that preserve expertise
5. Advocacy & Policy Engagement
• Contribute to institutional AI policies
• Advocate for responsible AI integration
• Participate in professional discourse about AI in education

Research Framework Alignment

Long & Magerko (2020): Implicit in "critical perspective"
Ng et al. (2021): "Ethical use of AI" (personal responsibility)
Cardon et al. (2023): "Professional use of AI" and "ethical considerations"
